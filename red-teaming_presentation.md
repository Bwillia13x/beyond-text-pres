# Red-Teaming Presentation Structure — v1

## Working thesis

We have entered a new era in postsecondary education because frontier AI is no longer just a text generator at the edge of academic work. It is increasingly a persistent, tool-using, workflow-shaping environment capable of completing meaningfully complex tasks across much of the digital work that students and faculty perform. As these capabilities continue to scale rapidly, assessment can no longer rely on polished artifacts alone as trustworthy evidence of understanding.

---

## Core opening strategy

Start broad, establish the capability frontier, then narrow into the assessment problem.

### Opening logic

1. Show the audience what frontier AI can do **today**.
2. Show that these capabilities are not isolated model tricks, but are increasingly embedded inside persistent environments and tool ecosystems.
3. Briefly show why the next 6–12 months are likely to intensify rather than reverse this trend.
4. Use that capability picture to establish the core conclusion: higher education has entered a new assessment era.
5. Only then pivot into red-teaming assignments as the practical response.

---

## Draft slide flow — condensed version

### Slide 1 — Title

**Red-Teaming Assignments: Designing for Valid Learning Evidence in the AI Era**

Opening line:
We are not dealing with a temporary disruption at the margins of teaching and learning. We are dealing with a structural shift in what digital systems can now do inside academic work.

---

### Slide 2 — Why start with capability?

Purpose:
Explain why the presentation begins with frontier AI itself rather than policy or cheating.

Key point:
Before discussing assessment redesign, the audience must first accept the premise that frontier AI can already perform a large share of the artifact production and workflow execution that postsecondary education has historically treated as evidence of student competence.

Transition line:
If we understate the capability frontier, we will understate the assessment problem.

---

### Slide 3 — Frontier AI today: models, environments, workflows

Purpose:
Collapse the prior model-tour and environment-tour into one disciplined slide.

Contents to combine:

* Representative frontier systems: GPT-5.2 / Codex, Claude Opus 4.6 / Claude Code, Gemini 3.1 Pro / NotebookLM
* Core convergences: stronger reasoning, multimodality, tool use, longer task horizons, persistent context
* Environment shift: from single prompts to project-based, agentic, resumable workflow systems

Speaker note:
Do not make this a product safari. The point is not which model is best. The point is that multiple frontier systems now converge on capabilities and environments that materially change academic work.

---

### Slide 4 — Why this matters to postsecondary education

Purpose:
Translate capability into institutional significance.

Key points:

* Much postsecondary work is computer-mediated artifact production
* These systems now operate across writing, synthesis, spreadsheets, slides, code, data analysis, visual generation, and workflow support
* The result is not just better output, but deeper workflow compression and delegation

Core line:
The real challenge is not just AI-generated text. It is AI-mediated workflow.

---

### Slide 5 — Threshold claim

Purpose:
State the conclusion of the opening section without a separate wow or future slide.

Core line:
We have crossed a threshold where frontier AI can now produce or substantially mediate much of the artifact work that higher education has traditionally used as evidence of learning.

Supporting line:
This is not a prevalence claim about every student or every course. It is a threshold claim about what is now practically possible.

---

### Slide 6 — Illustrative exploit scenario: the multi-syllabus thought experiment

Purpose:
Introduce the vivid, concrete case.

Core framing:
This is not a prevalence claim and not a how-to for students. It is an illustrative threshold-case showing what becomes plausible when frontier models are embedded in persistent, multi-agent environments.

---

### Slide 7 — The setup: day-one workflow compression

Purpose:
Show the structure of the exploit without becoming a procedural tutorial.

Suggested visual:
A simple diagram with 4–5 courses, each feeding into its own Codex / Claude-style project environment, each with multiple subagents branching off inside the project.

Key points:

* Student uploads syllabus, rubric, LMS handouts, lecture slides, readings, exemplar formats
* One persistent project per course
* Multiple subagents per course handle synthesis, deadline mapping, artifact drafting, slide support, spreadsheet work, coding, revision, etc.
* Projects run in parallel across the semester rather than one assignment at a time

---

### Slide 8 — What the exploit demonstrates

Purpose:
Name the educational significance of the thought experiment.

Core point:
The real shock is not speed alone. It is the possibility of artifact completion without corresponding understanding.

Suggested wording:
A student can increasingly externalize planning, synthesis, drafting, formatting, and even revision into persistent agent environments. The result is not just faster completion. It is the possibility of semester-long artifact production with learning no longer reliably coupled to output quality.

---

### Slide 9 — The old inference is breaking

Purpose:
Introduce artifact-only inference after the exploit has made the risk vivid.

Core point:
For much of modern higher education, the finished artifact functioned as a workable proxy for the understanding behind it. Under current conditions, that inference weakens.

Lead-in to next slide:
A polished artifact may now reflect deep understanding, shallow acceptance of AI-generated work, careful orchestration, or some combination.

---

### Slide 10 — The verification paradox

Purpose:
Introduce the central concept.

Core point:
AI is often most useful exactly where students are least equipped to verify what it produces.

Plain-English framing:
The tool expands capability fastest where auditability is weakest.

---

### Slide 11 — Red-teaming as the practical response

Purpose:
Pivot from diagnosis to action.

Core point:
Red-teaming means stress-testing assignments against current AI workflow conditions to see whether they still produce valid evidence of learning.

---

### Slide 12 — The four red-team questions

1. What is the assignment actually trying to make visible?
2. What kind of AI participation is it vulnerable to?
3. Where does the verification paradox appear?
4. What new evidence would restore stronger inference?

---

### Slide 13 — Example: generic essay

Purpose:
Walk through one high-clarity example.

---

### Slide 14 — Example redesign

Purpose:
Show a minimum-viable redesign, not a maximalist overhaul.

---

### Slide 15 — Student self-red-teaming

Purpose:
Extend red-teaming from faculty design practice to student metacognitive practice.

Core line:
A mature AI-era assessment strategy should not only red-team assignments against AI. It should also teach students to use AI to red-team their own assumptions, evidence, and reasoning under human judgment.

---

### Slide 16 — Participant activity

Prompt:
Red-team one of your own assignments.

---

### Slide 17 — Debrief

Purpose:
Surface patterns and show the problem is structural rather than idiosyncratic.

---

### Slide 18 — Final takeaway

Core line:
The goal is not AI-proofing. The goal is stronger evidence of learning under changed workflow conditions.

---

## Notes for next iteration

Questions to refine next:

1. How many slides should remain in the capability-setup section before the audience becomes impatient?
2. Which concrete examples should appear on the “wow” slide, and which should be cut?
3. How much explicit model naming should remain versus collapsing into broader capability categories?
4. What should the “next 6–12 months” slide say precisely without sounding speculative or breathless?
5. How visually explicit should the multi-syllabus thought experiment be without becoming a how-to exploit?
6. Do we want one large illustrative exploit case study in the middle or a shorter exploit slide followed immediately by red-teaming examples?
7. Should the exploit section include a screenshot / mockup of a multi-project Codex layout or remain diagrammatic?
